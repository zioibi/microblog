Aug 21, 2023

If chess isn’t your thing (yet), you would be forgiven for thinking that chess is chess. But even for games played by the normal rules of chess, there‚Äôs a huge variety of time controls that have dramatic impacts on play. You can, of course, play without any time limit, free to leave days between moves if you wish. Games at the FIDE World Cup ‚Äî which is in its final stages at the moment ‚Äî¬†are played with 90 minutes on each player‚Äôs clock for the first 40 moves, and an additional 30 minutes added after move 40. Also, 30 seconds are added per move. This is a version of the classical chess format, which has endured throughout the game‚Äôs long history. But other time controls have gained popularity, perhaps particularly since the advent of online chess. There is Rapid chess, where players might start with anything from 60 minutes to 10 minutes on the clock; Blitz, where clocks start with five or even three minutes, and sometimes two seconds might be added per move; and Bullet, where games can start with as little as 30 seconds on the clock!

But those are just the chess formats that use the normal rules. There are also numerous variations that tweak the way the game itself is played. For example, there is Chess960, wherein the order of pieces on each player‚Äôs home rank is randomised; Crazyhouse, in which pieces you capture can be redeployed on your side; and 4 Player Chess, in which‚Ä¶ there are four players, and four sets of pieces on one (larger) board. And that‚Äôs just the tip of the iceberg when it comes to chess variants.1

There has also been an explosion in the number and variety of chess video games, many of which feature fun spins on the game. See, as just two examples, Shotgun King: The Final Checkmate, and Zach Gage‚Äôs Really Bad Chess.

I mention all of this because I recently had an idea for a new variation, which I‚Äôm calling Meteorite Chess. The game uses the usual setup of chess pieces, and proceeds as a normal game of chess. The twist is that at set intervals (ie. every three or five moves) one square on the board becomes highlighted, and after the subsequent moves, that square is hit by a meteorite. I can see there being two versions of the game: Meteorite & Meteorite Oblivion. In the latter version, when the meteorite hits the highlighted square, any piece on that square is destroyed, and removed from the board. This is the simpler version of the game, but it still requires both players to adapt their play to accommodate squares which become temporarily hazardous. The game design trick would be in making sure that the squares were selected in a fair way. For example, no squares on the 1, 2, 7, or 8 ranks should probably be targeted at the beginning of the game. Perhaps they become eligible to be meteor struck after 30 moves, incentivising players to develop their pieces earlier. The fun of the game would be to embrace its randomness, but there could also be tweaks to guard against the feeling of unfairness ‚Äî¬†eg. a setting to provide two moves‚Äô worth of warning before a meteor strikes.

In the non-Oblivion version of the game, things get a little trickier, and perhaps a little more interesting. Here, instead of destroying the piece on the targeted square, the meteorite devolves it to a lesser piece. So, a struck queen becomes a rook; a rook becomes a knight or bishop; a bishop becomes a pawn; and a struck pawn is destroyed. In the majority of instances, this would still prove an incentive for players to avoid the targeted square, but it would give rise to fascinating board states where it might prove beneficial for a player to deliberately move a rook onto the highlighted square, because devolving it into a knight would provide a positional advantage! Again, there are options here to tweak the rules to make things more or less punishing: perhaps a setting that includes some devolving meteorites, and some that outright destroy, as well as the option to either indicate which type of meteorite is coming, or not. Heck, maybe there could even be evolving meteorites, which would increase struck pieces: knight to rook; rook to Queen etc.

As I‚Äôve said here before, I‚Äôm not a game designer. But if anyone reading this would like to make use of this idea, feel free! ‚ôüÔ∏è

Over the last five seasons, for pretty much every game of baseball that I watched, you’d find me wearing a replica Jason Heyward jersey. A lot has been written during that period about the wisdom of Heyward’s contract with the Cubs (eight years; $184M), and I don’t aim to add to it here. At his best, I always found Heyward a magnetic player to watch. His game-winning walk-off grand slam against the Phillies in 2018 is something I’ll never forget, and there are plenty of other highlights. Not to mention the consistently stunning defence that won him no fewer than five gold gloves, and the fact that he seems to be a genuinely wonderful person away from the diamond.

The Cubs having released him in November, I’m sure going to miss the guy this coming season.

Heyward’s departure sent me shopping for a new jersey. One consideration is longevity: no one wants to drop money on a shirt, only to find the player has been traded away from the club a season later. Following that logic, I might have picked up a jersey that read SUZUKI #27 (signed through 2026), or even SWANSON #7 (signed through 2029). In the end, however, I went a different direction. When you happen to share a surname with one of your team’s legends, sometimes you have to lean into it!

I could not be more excited to wear this at London Stadium this summer, rooting for the Cubbies in person, for the first time!
